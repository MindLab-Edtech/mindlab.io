# External Research — Verified Findings

> Produced 2026-07-05 by a deep-research pass (5 search angles, ~15 sources fetched, every claim
> adversarially verified by 3 independent checks; votes shown). These findings feed
> `LANDING-PAGE-STRATEGY.md`. Confidence and caveats are preserved — read them before quoting
> any number publicly.

## Summary

The research validates MindLab's positioning thesis end-to-end. The PE/M&A buyer already uses
GenAI, but every top adoption barrier is trust-shaped, and in practice finance users of leading
tools still manually re-verify AI outputs — so the page must lead objection-handling with
security, provenance, and human-review proof, not speed claims. Competitively, incumbents fight
over category labels and proprietary content breadth, and citations are already commoditized down
to a sentence-level granularity war — but **no major vendor claims a human review/approval gate
before AI output becomes source-of-truth**, making R3 a verified whitespace axis. For design,
Pentagram's finance portfolio proves restrained, reduction-first aesthetics in this vertical, and
Ramp's Bakken & Baeck "Bento box" system is the concrete precedent for visualizing a dense data
product via stylized, grid-disciplined product-UI abstractions.

Known gap: landing-page conversion *mechanics* (benchmarks, tested CTA phrasing) for high-ACV
finance buyers did not survive verification — treat that part of the strategy as informed craft,
not cited fact.

## Finding 1 — Buyer readiness and budget are proven [HIGH, 2-1 / 3-0]

86% of 1,000 senior US corporate and PE leaders surveyed H1 2025 have integrated GenAI into M&A
workflows; 65% of them within the past year. Among adopters, 83% invested $1M+ specifically for
M&A teams — including **88% of PE firms**. The landing page addresses buyers who are *already
adopting*, and who can support a high-ACV, implementation-first motion. But adoption is shallow
("early innings", concentrated pre-sign), so depth-of-workflow education still matters.

Caveats: self-reported; skews larger US orgs (mid-market PE adoption ~36% per adjacent coverage);
"$1M+ invested" includes internal builds/consulting, not only SaaS.

Sources:
- https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/what-we-do/capabilities/mergers-acquisitions-restructuring/articles/m-and-a-generative-ai-study.html
- https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/deloitte-survey-genai-in-mna.html

## Finding 2 — Top adoption barriers are all trust-shaped [HIGH, 3-0]

Data security (67%), data quality/availability (65%), model reliability (64%), ethical
considerations (62%), regulatory/compliance uncertainty (61%) — nearly tied, one trust cluster.
This is the strongest empirical mandate for making security posture, provenance/citations, human
review gates, and compliance/audit answers **first-class page sections, not footer badges**.

Sources: same Deloitte study + press release; corroborated by TechMonitor, Cybersecurity Dive.
FTI Consulting's 2026 PE AI Radar finds PE trails on AI governance/auditability — reinforcing.

## Finding 3 — Users of leading AI tools still re-verify everything [MEDIUM, 3-0 / 2-1]

Hebbia's own commissioned survey (510 US finance professionals, Apr–May 2026): **59% manually
verify or re-create AI outputs all or most of the time**, citing hallucinations and missing
citations — even among firms reporting "full AI trust," re-check rates stay at that level. A
PE-diligence software guide (transacted.io) reports Hebbia users "manually checking source files
because answer reliability and numeric accuracy can be uneven" and lists "traceability,
permissions, audit history, and governance controls required for institutional use" as core
buying criteria. SEC 2026 Examination Priorities make AI oversight/documentation an exam focus
for registered advisers (Reg S-P AI-vendor due diligence deadline June 3, 2026).

Pain-point narrative for the page: **the hidden cost of unverified AI is that your team re-does
the work.**

Caveat: transacted.io is a competitor-adjacent comparison blog — attribute carefully; the safer
public citations are Hebbia's own survey (admission against interest) and the SEC priorities.

Sources:
- https://www.transacted.io/blog/ai-private-equity-diligence-software
- https://www.hebbia.com/resources/diligence-paradox-survey

## Finding 4 — GenAI traction concentrates pre-sign, where Target Brief lives [HIGH, 3-0]

40% of adopters apply GenAI to M&A strategy/market assessment, 35% to target identification and
screening, 35% to due diligence (post-sign stages ~32%). Leading the page with outside-in
screening research meets buyers where adoption already is; Portfolio Pulse (post-sign monitoring)
is the less-crowded expansion story.

Sources: Deloitte (as above); directional pattern corroborated by Bain 2026 M&A report
(pre-deal GenAI investment 46% vs 27% post-deal).

## Finding 5 — The market is stuck in pilot mode [HIGH, 3-0]

Bain (Sept 2024 survey, investors representing $3.2T AUM): a majority of portfolio companies were
in some phase of GenAI testing but only **~20% had operationalized use cases with concrete
results**; "the preponderance of use cases remain in pilot mode." Validates the
implementation-first, design-partner motion and the copy angle **"from pilot to production /
system of record."** This buyer has seen demos; they have not seen production.

Caveat: measures portco adoption, not PE-firm internal tooling; dated snapshot — cite
time-anchored.

Source: https://www.bain.com/insights/field-notes-from-generative-ai-insurgency-global-private-equity-report-2025/

## Finding 6 — The category is fought over labels and content breadth, not workflow trust [HIGH, 3-0]

AlphaSense claims "enterprise-grade market intelligence and AI search platform," anchors on an
"exclusive collection of 10,000+" proprietary sources, and down-categorizes Hebbia as "a search
engine and LLM productivity tool… no proprietary data of its own." Hebbia segments the market
into "agentic operating system" (itself) vs "data-rich search engines" (AlphaSense, Capital IQ,
FactSet, Bloomberg) vs "precision vertical specialists" (Model ML, Datarails, Aiera, DataSnipper).

Implication: **do not compete on content breadth or adopt these contested labels** — a distinct
category framing avoids being down-positioned inside either vendor's taxonomy.

Sources:
- https://www.alpha-sense.com/compare/hebbia-alternatives/
- https://www.hebbia.com/resources/rogo-competitors

## Finding 7 — Citations are commoditized; the human approval gate is unclaimed whitespace [HIGH, 3-0]

AlphaSense markets snippet-level citations; Hebbia attacks Rogo on citation *granularity*
("lacks true sentence-level sourcing") and markets sentence-level citations; Rogo markets
"auditable citations"; F2/V7 compete on cell-level precision. Verifiers exhaustively term-searched
competitor marketing surfaces for human/review/approve/verify/oversight language applied to AI
outputs and **found none** — no major vendor's primary marketing claims human review/approval
before AI output becomes a record.

Copy implication: do not lead with "cited answers" (parity); **lead with "nothing becomes a
record until a human approves it,"** with citations as supporting proof.

Scope caveat: verified on primary marketing surfaces of AlphaSense, Hebbia, Rogo + V7/F2 spot
checks; Keye, Eilla, BlueFlame, Grata, Affinity, DealCloud not individually verified.

Sources: as Finding 6, live-fetched 2026-07-05.

## Finding 8 — Peer-reviewed evidence supports R3's core mechanic [HIGH, 3-0]

A 2026 systematic review (Lazaros, Vrahatis & Kotsiantis, *Entropy* 28:377, PRISMA-aligned) finds
verification-based HITL workflows — AI generates drafts, humans review/correct/refine — "can
reduce the time spent… while preserving or improving the quality," provided reviewers guard
against automation bias; adoption is strongest under **calibrated trust** (over-trust and
under-trust both degrade performance). Corroborated by Vaccaro et al. 2024 (*Nature Human
Behaviour* meta-analysis, 106 studies): human-AI combos show significant gains specifically in
content-creation tasks (AI-drafts/human-refines).

Messaging implication: sell **"appropriately verifiable AI"** — transparent provenance, review
gates, visible uncertainty — not "AI you can blindly trust." The review workflow itself is the
anti-automation-bias feature, framable as evidence-backed rather than marketing.

Caveats: empirical settings are annotation/teaching materials, not PE research — cite at the
workflow-pattern level only; do not extrapolate time-savings numbers.

Source: https://www.mdpi.com/1099-4300/28/4/377

## Finding 9 — Pentagram's language is field-proven in finance [HIGH, 3-0]

Pentagram's finance portfolio (~26 projects) includes PayPal (2024 refresh), Mastercard
(Bierut/Hayman — elements "isolated… to their purest form"), American Express (Abbott Miller,
2018), Citibank (Paula Scher, 1998, still in use), plus Bloomberg, Nubank, Banco Itaú. Its own
framing emphasizes **reduction over ornament** — validating MindLab's restrained/editorial,
anti-AI-slop direction with concrete reference identities.

Caveat: brand identity work ≠ landing-page design — treat as aesthetic north star (typography,
reduction, grid discipline), not a page-pattern library.

Source: https://www.pentagram.com/finance

## Finding 10 — Ramp's "Bento box" system is the precedent for visualizing a dense product [HIGH, 3-0]

Ramp's product graphic system by Bakken & Baeck: a flexible modular grid "built to adapt to both
low-density visuals (such as abstract images) and high-density visuals… which more closely
reflect the product's UI," with the stated purpose of bridging "the gap between Ramp's brand and
product." Direction for MindLab: represent Target Brief / Portfolio Pulse / R3 through
**stylized, grid-disciplined UI abstractions and workflow diagrams that echo real product
density** — one unified visual system spanning editorial brand moments and product proof — rather
than raw screenshots or generic 3D "AI" art.

Caveats: agency self-published case study — authoritative for intent, not measured conversion
effect.

Source: https://bakkenbaeck.com/case/ramp
